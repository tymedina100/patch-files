Regulatory timeline preview

The EU AI Act applies on a phased schedule: prohibited AI practices and AI-literacy obligations began applying on 2 February 2025; general-purpose AI model obligations began applying on 2 August 2025; most remaining obligations, including many high-risk AI-system obligations, apply from 2 August 2026; and obligations for certain high-risk AI systems covered by Annex I apply from 2 August 2027.

Last updated: 2 July 2026. Sources: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Article 113; European Commission AI Act materials. See EUR-Lex official text and European Commission AI Act overview.

The EU AI Act includes administrative fine thresholds that can reach up to €35 million or 7% of total worldwide annual turnover for certain prohibited-practice infringements, with different maximums for other infringement categories.

Last updated: 2 July 2026. Source: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Article 99. See EUR-Lex official text.

In the United States, AI governance requirements remain fragmented across state, local, privacy, consumer-protection, employment, and sector-specific regimes rather than one comprehensive federal AI statute for private-sector AI governance.

Last updated: 2 July 2026. Sources for examples of fragmented U.S. requirements: Colorado SB24-205/SB25B-004, New York City Local Law 144 of 2021, and FTC business guidance on AI claims and unfair/deceptive practices. See Colorado General Assembly SB25B-004, NYC DCWP AEDT guidance, and FTC AI claims guidance.

Colorado's Consumer Protections for Artificial Intelligence Act requirements were extended to take effect no later than 30 June 2026, creating obligations for developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems used in consequential decisions.

Last updated: 2 July 2026. Source: Colorado General Assembly, SB25B-004 bill summary extending SB24-205 requirements to 30 June 2026. See Colorado General Assembly SB25B-004.

New York City Local Law 144 restricts employer and employment-agency use of automated employment decision tools unless bias-audit, public-summary, and notice requirements are satisfied.

Last updated: 2 July 2026. Source: New York City Department of Consumer and Worker Protection AEDT guidance. See NYC DCWP Automated Employment Decision Tools page.

Executive summary

Mid-market technology companies are entering a decisive AI governance window. The EU AI Act's 2025, 2026, and 2027 phased application dates make governance planning a current operating issue for teams that place AI systems on the EU market, put AI systems into service in the EU, or have AI-system outputs used in the EU.

Last updated: 2 July 2026. Sources: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Articles 2 and 113. See EUR-Lex official text.

For SaaS, HR tech, fintech, health tech, and enterprise AI vendors, the immediate commercial risk is not only regulator action. It is deal friction. Enterprise buyers increasingly ask vendors to document AI use, explain vendor and model dependencies, describe oversight for high-impact workflows, and show how AI-related incidents or errors are escalated.

This report recommends a practical readiness program built around four outcomes: know what AI is in use, know which systems create regulated or high-impact risk, know what evidence customers and auditors will ask for, and know who owns each mitigation.
